Understanding High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)

Published: January 2026 | Health Education | Read time: 5 minutes

⚠️ Medical Disclaimer: This article provides general health information and does not replace professional medical advice. Always consult with a healthcare provider for diagnosis and treatment.

What is High Blood Pressure?

Blood pressure is the force with which your heart pumps blood through your arteries. It's measured in two numbers: systolic pressure (the higher number) and diastolic pressure (the lower number). For example, a reading of 120/80 mmHg (millimeters of mercury) is considered normal.

High blood pressure (hypertension) occurs when blood pressure consistently stays at 130/80 mmHg or higher. This is a serious condition because the extra force on your artery walls can damage them over time, increasing the risk of heart disease, stroke, and kidney problems.

Why is Hypertension Dangerous?

  • Silent Damage: High blood pressure often has no symptoms, earning it the nickname "the silent killer"
  • Cardiovascular Stress: Your heart has to work harder, which can lead to heart disease and heart attacks
  • Stroke Risk: Elevated pressure can damage blood vessels in the brain, increasing stroke risk
  • Kidney Damage: High blood pressure can harm the kidneys, affecting their ability to filter waste
  • Vision Problems: Hypertension can damage blood vessels in the eyes

What Causes High Blood Pressure?

Primary Hypertension (Most Common - 90-95% of cases)

Often develops gradually over many years. Risk factors include:

  • Age (risk increases with age)
  • Family history of high blood pressure
  • Being overweight or obese
  • Sedentary lifestyle (lack of physical activity)
  • High salt intake
  • Excessive alcohol consumption
  • Chronic stress
  • Sleep problems

Secondary Hypertension (5-10% of cases)

Caused by an underlying health condition or medication:

  • Kidney disease
  • Thyroid problems
  • Adrenal gland disorders
  • Certain medications
  • Pregnancy-related conditions

Recognizing Symptoms

Most people with high blood pressure have no symptoms at all. This is why regular monitoring is crucial. When symptoms do occur, they may include:

  • Headaches
  • Shortness of breath
  • Nosebleeds
  • Chest pain
  • Dizziness

Note: These symptoms are non-specific and could indicate other conditions. Always see a healthcare provider for proper diagnosis.

Lifestyle Changes for Blood Pressure Management

1. Reduce Sodium (Salt) Intake

  • Aim for less than 2,300 mg of sodium per day (about 1 teaspoon of salt)
  • Reduce processed foods, which contain most of our dietary salt
  • Season food with herbs and spices instead of salt

2. Increase Physical Activity

  • Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week
  • Examples: brisk walking, cycling, swimming
  • Gradual increase is better than sudden intense exercise

3. Maintain a Healthy Weight

  • Losing even 5-10% of body weight can reduce blood pressure
  • Focus on balanced nutrition and portion control

4. Limit Alcohol

  • Men: No more than 2 drinks per day
  • Women: No more than 1 drink per day

5. Manage Stress

  • Practice relaxation techniques (deep breathing, meditation)
  • Regular physical activity helps reduce stress
  • Ensure adequate sleep (7-9 hours per night)

6. Eat a Heart-Healthy Diet

  • DASH Diet: Emphasizes vegetables, fruits, whole grains, lean proteins, and low-fat dairy
  • Rich in potassium (bananas, sweet potatoes, spinach)
  • Reduce saturated fats and trans fats

Regular Blood Pressure Monitoring

Everyone should have their blood pressure checked regularly:

  • Adults 18-39: At least once per year
  • Adults 40+: Every year or as recommended
  • People with hypertension: As directed by healthcare provider

When to See a Healthcare Provider

  • For a baseline blood pressure reading
  • If your blood pressure is consistently elevated (130/80 or higher)
  • If you experience symptoms like severe headaches, chest pain, or shortness of breath
  • If you've been diagnosed with hypertension and need medication management
  • For regular monitoring if you have risk factors

Key Takeaways

  • High blood pressure is a serious condition that often has no symptoms
  • Regular monitoring is crucial for early detection
  • Lifestyle changes can significantly impact blood pressure levels
  • Professional medical care is essential for diagnosis and treatment
  • Managing hypertension reduces your risk of heart disease, stroke, and kidney damage
